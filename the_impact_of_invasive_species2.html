The Impact of Invasive Species

A Invasive species are among the leading threats to the native wildlife of most countries, with approximately 42 percent of endangered species at risk from them. Invasive species can be any kind of organism – for example, a mammal, amphibian, fish, insect or plant – that is not native to an ecosystem. Often they reproduce and spread with great speed. Contrary to popular belief, a plant or animal classified as an invasive species does not necessarily originate in another country. For example, lake trout are native to the Great Lakes of North America, but are considered to be an invasive species in Yellowstone Lake in Wyoming because they compete with native cutthroat trout for habitat.

B When a new species is introduced into an ecosystem, native wildlife may struggle to compete with it for food and other resources. Invasive species can change the food web in an ecosystem by destroying or replacing native food sources, while providing little or no food for local wildlife. In extreme cases, the invader may prey on the native species. Above all, invasive species threaten biodiversity in many habitats. For instance, the climbing plant species kudzu (which is native to East Asia) can easily replace a habitat that had a wide range of plants with a monoculture, consisting solely of kudzu, as has started to happen in the south east of the USA.

C The phenomenon is not an exclusively modern one. Humans have always transferred a variety of species from one region to another, but the development of rapid means of transportation has increased the frequency of such introductions. Aquatic organisms can be shipped across the oceans, while insects can easily get into the wooden crates that are transported around the world in this way. In addition, climate change has enabled some invasive plant species to colonise new areas. Other invasive species include pets such as snakes or turtles which are intentionally set free into the wild.

D Invasive species do not all present the same level of threat to native ecosystems and can be classified into three types. The first of these may be introduced species which can maintain themselves in a limited range of habitats without upsetting the ecological equilibrium of the area. Some scientists have even argued that in these cases the introduction enhances the diversity of animal and plant life in that specific area. A second group of invaders present a greater threat because their spread is at the expense of one native species. The North American grey squirrel, for example, was introduced to the UK in 1878 largely because wealthy landowners thought it would be a fashionable and attractive addition to the local wildlife on their estates. It spread widely, leading directly to the dramatic decline in the population of the native red squirrel. However, it would appear that this is the only definite negative impact of the grey squirrel.

E There is a third level of threat in which the dominance of the introduced species has an extremely destructive effect on the entire ecosystem. One of the most damaging examples involved a species of comb jellyfish. Native to estuaries along the western Atlantic coast from the northern United States to the Valdés peninsula in Argentina, this species was released by a ship into the Black Sea in Eastern Europe in the early 1980s. Within six years, the population had exploded, transforming the entire ecosystem and causing a dramatic decline in fish numbers. The jellyfish consumed vast quantities of plankton, leaving little for other species, and in turn the decline in fish stocks affected local fishing industries and the birds and mammals that relied on fish. The comb jellyfish caused more damage to the Black Sea than even industrial pollution from factories.

F Another highly invasive species is the water hyacinth, which was brought to North America in the late nineteenth century as an ornamental plant for a horticultural exhibition in 1884. This floating plant forms dense mats that block sunlight and reduce oxygen levels in the water, making it difficult for aquatic life to survive. It has spread rapidly across North America, damaging boat engines and blocking cooling pipes for power plants, which has led to blackouts. Its impact on native water plants is largely unstudied, as is the case with most ecosystems that are invaded by new plant species.

G Although invasive species are a major environmental issue, ordinary people have a limited understanding of the threat they pose. As a result, less attention is paid to this problem than to other environmental concerns such as climate change or deforestation. Education is therefore essential to address the threat, as are changes to the way in which international trade and transport operate, since these are major pathways for the spread of invasive species.

H There are practical steps that individuals and governments can take to limit the spread of invasive species. Planting native species in gardens rather than non-native ornamental plants can help. Similarly, cleaning hiking or camping equipment thoroughly before moving to a new area can prevent the accidental transfer of seeds or insects. Any sightings of invasive species should be reported to local wildlife or agricultural organizations so that they can be monitored and, if necessary, removed. Governments can introduce tighter controls on shipping and trade to prevent accidental introductions, as well as fund programmes to remove invasive species from affected areas.
